Why Cyber Safety Matters

In our digital world, almost everything — banking, education, healthcare, and communication — happens online. A single breach can lead to financial loss, identity theft, or even national security risks.

Real-Life Cyber Attacks (2025 Examples)

Marks & Spencer Ransomware (April 2025)

Social engineering on third-party contractors → ransomware disrupted online orders for weeks. Estimated loss: £300 million.

University Phishing Breaches

Harvard, Penn, Princeton hit via phone/email phishing → personal data of alumni and students stolen.

Supply Chain Ransomware

Attacks on distributors like UNFI and Ingram Micro disrupted food & IT supply chains.

Lesson: Most attacks start with human error (phishing/social engineering).

Goals of Cybersecurity: The CIA Triad

Confidentiality

Keeping data private. Only authorized people can access it.

Example: Your bank account details

Integrity

Data remains accurate and unaltered.

Example: Exam marks not changed by hacker

Availability

Data and systems available when needed.

Example: Website not downed by DDoS

Threat vs Vulnerability vs Risk

Threat

A potential danger (e.g., a hacker, virus, or ransomware attacker).

Vulnerability

A weakness (e.g., weak password, unpatched software, or open port).

Risk

The likelihood + impact when a threat exploits a vulnerability.
Risk = Threat × Vulnerability

Firewall

Blocks unauthorized network traffic

Antivirus

Detects and removes malware

Malware

Malicious software (virus, trojan, etc.)

Hacking

Unauthorized access (ethical vs malicious)

Introduction to Cryptography

Symmetric Encryption

Same key for encrypt & decrypt (fast but key sharing is risky). Example: AES

Asymmetric Encryption

Public key to encrypt, private key to decrypt. Example: RSA, used in HTTPS
